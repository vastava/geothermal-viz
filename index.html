Geothermal Energy: The solution right under our feet


Geothermal energy takes advantage of the biggest energy storage system on Earth — the Earth itself. The center of the Earth is as hot as the surface of the sun (6000 °C), and with each year, enough heat flows to Earth's surface to meet total global energy consumption twice-over.

On top of that, the geothermal reservoir is boundless — heat within 10km of Earth's surface has roughly 50,000 times more energy than all fossil fuel resources worldwide. And yet, geothermal energy makes up less than 1% of global installed electricity capacity.

Introduction


Geothermal power plants use hydrothermal resources that have both water (hydro) and heat (thermal). Geothermal power plants require high-temperature (300°F to 700°F) hydrothermal resources that come from either dry steam wells or from hot water wells. People use these resources by drilling wells into the earth and then piping steam or hot water to the surface. The hot water or steam powers a turbine that generates electricity. Some geothermal wells are as much as two miles deep.

The United States leads the world in terms of electricity generated by geothermal energy. There are power plants in seven states, which, in 2018, produced about 16.7 billion kilowatthours — amounting to about 0.4% of total U.S. utility-scale electricity generation.

West Coast


Most of the geothermal power plants in the United States are in western states and Hawaii, where geothermal energy resources are close to the earth's surface. California generates the most electricity from geothermal energy. The Geysers dry steam reservoir in Northern California is the largest known dry steam field in the world and has been producing electricity since 1960.
